Investigating Open Alerts

In the summary Patient Workflow, each patient with open alerts has a flag to allow timely detection and resolution. The Open Alerts flag is one of CM’s most important status indicators.

 

A provider alert is a real time prompt; the information in an alert encourages a physician to make a timely, informed decision for the care of a specific patient.  Although our primary audience is physicians, other providers are expected to use the alerts including physician assistants, nurse practitioners, pharmacists and other clinicians.  The queries within the underling logic; that is, the analytic rule that invokes the alert, select a group of patients with conditions that may warrant the specified intervention. Alerts tracking is a powerful tool in maintaining optimal levels of prevention and care. Alerts logging also helps you to document care given and demonstrate your care team’s eligility for incentive payments (both in the Care Coordination program and in other CMS-endorsed care quality programs).

 

 

CCM uses a universal warning signal to alert the Care Manager or Care Physician know when a patient condition has become time sensitive. Open alerts may indicate patients whose lab values or other clinical parameters approach a threshold. Open alerts may also indicate approaching deadlines in care-plan compliance. For example, to quality for some incentive payments, the provider may need to administer—and document—a specified intervention before a deadline arrives. These deadlines are part of best practices in clinical care.

 

To access a  clinical alert specific alert, click on its name in the Alerts column, then click Edit, to update any necessary investigate open alerts and edit alert records.

 

 

The alert name links to a window (popup) showing details of the alert.

 

 

The alert detail display lists the measure number of one or more the clinical quality measures. Each measure has a role in a larger incentive program. If the provider is to gain the credit (and any associated incentive payment or credit) for the measure in question, the user or provider must comply with the measure and close the alert. For example, measure-compliant care for a patient with hypertension may include an appointment every three months to check the patient’s blood pressure and review his or her dosage of antihypertensive medication. The care manager or care physician would make an appropriate response to the alert, such as reminding the patient to schedule a prompt appointment, or documenting any such appointment that has taken place within the timeframe.  The user, if authorized, could close the alert and secure the Clinical Quality Measure (CQM) incentive payment or credit.

 

Note: The Center for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) maintains several incentive programs. The CQM incentives may not be the same as the Chronic Care Management incentive payments that relate to care-coordination interview.
